Step 1: contract each block — they collapse to only three distinct polynomials

Do the six‑dot contraction of each named tensor directly from its index pattern (pure bookkeeping, using $\mathrm{e}_a\mathrm{e}_b \to M_{ba}$):

$$ \begin{aligned} \mathsf{i}^3\vdots\vdots\mathsf{M}^3 &= \textstyle\sum_{pqr} M_{pp}M_{qq}M_{rr}\\ \mathfrak{I}\mathsf{i}\vdots\vdots\mathsf{M}^3 = \mathsf{i}\mathfrak{I}\vdots\vdots\mathsf{M}^3 = Б\vdots\vdots\mathsf{M}^3 &= \textstyle\sum_{pqr} M_{pq}M_{qp}M_{rr}\\ Ш\vdots\vdots\mathsf{M}^3 &= \textstyle\sum_{pqr} M_{pq}M_{qr}M_{rp} \end{aligned} $$

The crucial observation is the middle line: $\mathfrak{I}\mathsf{i}$, $\mathsf{i}\mathfrak{I}$, and Б all contract to the exact same polynomial. They differ only in which of the three dyad factors carries the lone self‑paired $\mathsf{i}$ (innermost / outermost / middle, respectively), while the other two get cross‑linked — and since $\mathsf{M}^3=\mathsf{MMM}$ treats its three factors identically, the contraction can't tell them apart.

So if we write $\text{Д}=\alpha\mathsf{i}^3+\beta\mathfrak{I}\mathsf{i}+\gamma\mathsf{i}\mathfrak{I}+\deltaБ+\epsilonШ$, the contraction depends on only three numbers:

$$\text{Д}\vdots\vdots\mathsf{M}^3=\alpha\textstyle\sum M_{pp}M_{qq}M_{rr}+(\beta+\gamma+\delta)\sum M_{pq}M_{qp}M_{rr}+\epsilon\sum M_{pq}M_{qr}M_{rp}.$$

This already tells you the determinant cannot fix $\beta,\gamma,\delta$ individually — only their sum.

Step 2: match like terms against Sarrus

Now just compare the coefficient of three representative monomials with $\det = aei+dhc+gbf-ceg-fha-ibd$:

  • $M_{11}M_{22}M_{33}$ (the $aei$ term, coeff $+1$): only $\sum M_{pp}M_{qq}M_{rr}$ produces it, with multiplicity $6$ (the $3!$ orderings of $p,q,r$). The other two sums need a repeated index and contribute $0$. So $6\alpha=1.$
  • $M_{12}M_{23}M_{31}$ (a $gbf$‑type cyclic term, coeff $+1$): only $\sum M_{pq}M_{qr}M_{rp}$ produces it, with multiplicity $3$ (the cyclic rotations of $p,q,r$). So $3\epsilon=1.$
  • $M_{12}M_{21}M_{33}$ (an $ibd$‑type term, coeff $-1$): only $\sum M_{pq}M_{qp}M_{rr}$ produces it, with multiplicity $2$ (the pair $M_{12}M_{21}$ vs $M_{21}M_{12}$). So $2(\beta+\gamma+\delta)=-1.$

Hence

$$\alpha=\tfrac16,\qquad \beta+\gamma+\delta=-\tfrac12,\qquad \epsilon=\tfrac13,$$

with no trace identity ever invoked — just counting how many index assignments land on each monomial. (Equivalently, you could plug in $\mathrm{diag}(1,1,1)$, $\mathrm{diag}(1,1,0)$, $\mathrm{diag}(1,0,0)$ and solve the resulting $3\times3$ system; same result.)

Step 3: split the middle coefficient by symmetry

The determinant has done all it can. The last step is the symmetric form you're aiming for: since $\mathfrak{I}\mathsf{i}$, $\mathsf{i}\mathfrak{I}$, Б are just the three placements of the self‑paired factor among three interchangeable slots, there is no reason to favor any one of them, so Д should weight them equally:

$$\beta=\gamma=\delta=-\tfrac16.$$

Putting it together:

$$\boxed{\text{Д}=\tfrac16\mathsf{i}^3-\tfrac16\big(\mathfrak{I}\mathsf{i}+\mathsf{i}\mathfrak{I}+Б\big)+\tfrac13Ш}$$

Two nice payoffs worth noting in the text:

  • This is exactly the member of the family that also satisfies $\operatorname{adj}(\mathsf{M})=3\text{Д}::\mathsf{M}^2$ from the earlier discussion — the symmetry you impose here is the same symmetry the adjugate relation requires, so you get adjugate‑compatibility "for free" rather than as a separate axiom. You can frame the symmetry choice as: "we want the three factors of $\mathsf{M}^3$ on equal footing," and then later reveal that this is precisely what makes the adjugate contraction work.
